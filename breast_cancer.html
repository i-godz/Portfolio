    My Role

    Breast cancer is one of the most common forms of cancer among women worldwide. Early detection and accurate diagnosis play a crucial role in improving patient outcomes. In this project, I aim to develop a machine learning model that can accurately classify breast cancer tumors as either malignant or benign.

    Secondly, I obtained my dataset from Kaggle, which consists of a comprehensive collection of breast cancer cases. The dataset includes features such as radius mean, texture mean, perimeter mean, area mean, concavity mean, symmetry mean, and more. We imported the dataset into a pandas DataFrame, to ensure reliable and unbiased results, we carefully preprocessed the data by handling missing values, normalizing the features, and removing any duplicate or irrelevant entries. Furthermore, we encoded the diagnosis labels, replacing 'M' (Malignant) with [0] and 'B' (Benign) with [1]. Additionally, I splited the dataset into training and testing subsets to evaluate the performance of our models effectively.

    Thirdly, to gain insights into the dataset and visualize the distribution of diagnoses, we used matplotlib and seaborn libraries. I created a bar chart showing the diagnosis distribution, labeling the counts for each diagnosis type. Additionally, we generated a pair plot and a correlation heatmap to examine the relationships between different features and their correlation with the diagnosis. These visualizations help us understand the data and identify potential patterns or trends.

    Fourthly, I implemented three different machine learning models for brain tumor classification: Random Forest, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), and Support Vector Machine (SVM). For each model, we split the dataset into training and testing sets using a 80:20 ratio. The Random Forest model was trained using the RandomForestClassifier, while KNN and SVM models were trained using the KNeighborsClassifier and SVC classes, respectively. We calculated the training and testing accuracy scores for each model to evaluate their performance. The Random Forest model achieved a training accuracy of 100% and a testing accuracy of 100%. Similarly, the KNN model achieved a training accuracy of 90% and a testing accuracy of 94%. Finally, the SVM model using a linear kernel achieved a training accuracy of 100% and a testing accuracy of 100%.

    Fifthly, the machine learning models utilized for breast cancer classification yielded remarkable results. The Random Forest model achieved a training accuracy of 1.0 and a test accuracy of 1.0, indicating its robustness in accurately classifying breast cancer cases. The classification report further confirms the model's performance, showing high precision, recall, and F1-score for both the malignant and benign diagnoses. The KNN model also exhibited decent accuracy, with a training accuracy of 0.9011 and a test accuracy of 0.9386. Additionally, the linear kernel SVM model achieved a perfect training and test accuracy of 1.0, while the Gaussian kernel SVM model achieved a training accuracy of 0.8593 and a test accuracy of 0.9211.

    Finally, this project successfully developed machine learning-based models for breast cancer detection and classification. The Random Forest model demonstrated exceptional accuracy and precision, making it a reliable choice for breast cancer classification. The KNN and SVM models also provided good performance, offering alternative approaches for breast cancer diagnosis. These results showcase the potential of machine learning in assisting medical professionals in accurately identifying breast cancer cases and guiding appropriate treatment decisions.
